Problem Statement

Challenges for Berlin's Transport Systems

Climate Change in Berlin

  • 42% increase in extreme weather events since 2000
  • 2018 heatwave: €430M in transport damages
  • Summer heat >35°C (vs. 28°C in 1990)
  • Heavy rainfall events +25% since 2015

Systemic Vulnerability

  • U-Bahn tunnels heat 30% faster than surface
  • Behavioral adaptations: Passengers avoid uncomfortable conditions
  • Three key events in study period:
    • 2006: Heatwave (Ø 23.3°C in July)
    • 2010: Snow Chaos (-14°C in December)
    • 2013: Flood of the Century (141.6mm in July)
9%
Decline in U-Bahn usage at >28°C
Berlin's unique geographical position as a low-lying city with extensive underground infrastructure intensifies climate risks

Precipitation Effects

Gradient Boosting Methodology

Model selection and implementation

Why Gradient Boosting?

Data Challenge GBM Advantage Relevance to Resilience
Non-linear weather effects Automatic feature interaction detection Captures tipping points (e.g., ridership collapse above 30°C)
Small sample size (n=108) Robust to overfitting via shrinkage Prevents false resilience signals in sparse data
Temporal autocorrelation Handles lagged variables natively Models cascading demand shocks from multi-day storms

Model Implementation

  • Separate models for Bus and U-Bahn
  • Weather variables: Temperature, Precipitation, Sunshine
  • Temporal features: Month, Season
  • Lagged variables (Lag1) for demand persistence

Hyperparameters

  • Number of trees (n.trees): 150
  • Maximum tree depth (interaction.depth): 4
  • Learning rate (shrinkage): 0.07
  • Random seed: set.seed(123)

Temporal patterns and demand behavior

Seasonal Patterns

Modal Differences

  • U-Bahn: Relatively stable demand
  • Causes:

    • Bus more exposed to weather conditions

Weather Impact on Ridership

Quantifying climate effects on transport demand

Temperature Effects

  • U-Bahn: 9% decline at >28°C
  • Bus: 5.8% decline at >28°C
  • U-Bahn sensitivity 3.2% higher than bus
  • Reasons: Thermal comfort, ventilation limits
0.7%
Demand decline per +1°C >25°C (U-Bahn)

Precipitation Effects

  • Paradoxical effect: 5-7% increase in bus ridership
  • Behavioral shift: Switching from bicycle/walking to bus
  • U-Bahn: Less impact due to sheltered access
  • Non-linear relationship

Model Performance Evaluation

Accuracy assessment of predictive models

Evaluation Metrics

  • MAE (Mean Absolute Error): Average absolute error
  • RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error): Penalizes larger errors
  • 70-30 split between training and test data

Results:

Bus MAE: 1.45M | RMSE: 1.63M

U-Bahn MAE: 1.52M | RMSE: 1.72M

Performance Analysis

  • Bus model: Lower average errors
  • U-Bahn model: Higher RMSE due to some outliers
  • Both models show acceptable accuracy
  • Improvement potential with larger datasets

Key Observations

  • Error distribution not completely uniform
  • U-Bahn model shows higher sensitivity to extreme values
  • Complex but similar behavioral patterns in both systems
  • RMSE values acceptable for strategic planning

Variable Importance Analysis

Key drivers of transport demand

Most Influential Factors

  • Average temperature: Dominant predictor for both systems
  • Lagged demand (Lag1): Second most important factor
  • Bus: Sunshine hours, month and season significant
  • U-Bahn: Less seasonal influence due to sheltered infrastructure

Partial Dependence Plots

  • Non-linear temperature effect with tipping points
  • Strong positive autocorrelation in lagged demand
  • Month effect in U-Bahn: Increase in months 8-10 (post-summer return)
  • Bus: Non-linear effect of sunshine hours

Strategic Implications

  • Temperature and past demand must be core to any planning model
  • Autocorrelation shows value of subscription models
  • Seasonal patterns require flexible resource allocation
  • Monthly predictions improve maintenance planning
